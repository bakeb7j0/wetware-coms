## Prompt:
is there an adjective form of the noun obverse?

## Response:
Yes — but it’s a little tricky.

The noun **obverse** (the front face of a coin, medal, or figuratively the “counterpart” of something) doesn’t have a widely used “official” adjective form like _obversive_ in everyday English. Instead:

*   **Obverse** itself can be used adjectivally:  
    _“The obverse side of the coin bears the emperor’s head.”_  
    In this sense, it works exactly like _reverse_ — both nouns double as adjectives when describing surfaces or sides.
*   There _is_ a rarer adjective **obversive**, but you’ll mostly find it in specialized or dated contexts (philosophy, numismatics, or logic), where it means “relating to obversion” (a logical operation) rather than “front-facing.”

So:

*   If you’re talking coins, medals, tokens, or figurative “two sides of a thing,” just use **obverse** as an adjective.
*   If you’re talking logic or want to sound like you swallowed an OED, you can reach for **obversive**—but expect people to look it up.
